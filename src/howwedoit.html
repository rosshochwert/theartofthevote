    How We Do It
    How We Do It
    The Senate is comprised of 100 Senators, two for each state. Each one is elected to a six-year term by popular vote within their state. Fun fact: There are no term limits.

    Senators are divided into three classes and their terms are staggered so each class is up for reelection every two years. In 2020, there are 33 elections from Senate Class 2, along with two special elections — Arizona to replace the late Senator John McCain and Georgia to replace Senator Johnny Isakson after his retirement in December 2019.


    A Quick Recap:
    • The Senate currently has 53 Republicans and 47 Democrats (including the two Independents who caucus with the Democrats)
    • Republicans are defending 23 seats out of 53 seats
    • Democrats are defending 12 out of 47 seats
    • All the seats Democrats are defending in 2020 are in solidly blue states, except for two
    • Democrats need to gain three or four seats — if we hold all the seats we currently have — to flip the Senate
    • The Vice President of the United States serves as President of the Senate and casts the decisive vote in the event of a tie
    How You Do It
    Senators are reelected every six years, and elections are staggered so 1/3 of the Senate is up for reelection every even calendar year. That means in 2020, Republicans are defending 23 seats and Democrats are defending 12 seats. To halt — and repair — the damage caused by McConnell’s Senate, your vote counts more than ever.

    Fast Facts:
    • The Senate currently has 53 Republicans and 47 Democrats (If we include the two Independents).
    • All the seats Democrats are defending in 2020 are in solidly blue states, except for two.
    • Democrats need to gain four seats in November’s election or they need to gain three and win back the White House, which would allow the vice president to cast tie breaking votes.
